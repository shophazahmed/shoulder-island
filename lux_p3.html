02 Executive Summary
▼

Seaweed farming is one of the most capital-efficient and environmentally restorative forms of aquaculture. Shoulder Island's lagoon provides ideal warm, nutrient-rich conditions for cultivating Eucheuma and Sargassum species used in food, cosmetics, and fertiliser industries. By integrating fish cages and shellfish (IMTA), the system achieves nutrient cycling that reduces waste, boosts fish yields, and qualifies for blue carbon credits under the Verra Verified Carbon Standard.

Revenue streams include dry seaweed export (MVR 5,390/tonne), IMTA fish sales to resorts, and carbon credit sales. The operation requires minimal infrastructure and scales with the natural lagoon ecosystem.

03 System Design & Operations
▼

Seaweed Longlines

  • Species: Eucheuma cottonii (kappaphycus) for carrageenan export, Sargassum for fertiliser
  • Longline grid: 500m initial + 2km Year 3 expansion in lagoon shallows
  • 45-day harvest cycles, 6–8 harvests per year
  • Dry yield ratio: 8:1 (wet:dry), selling at MVR 5,390/tonne dry weight

IMTA Cages

  • Fish species: Grouper, Snapper, Pompano — high resort demand, premium prices
  • Shellfish: Pearl oysters and sea cucumbers in lower cage layers
  • Fish waste feeds seaweed below; seaweed oxygenates fish water — closed nutrient loop
  • Integration with hydroponics: nutrient-rich water from fish tanks irrigates grow beds

Carbon Credits (Verra VCS)

  • Seaweed absorbs CO₂ during growth — qualifies for blue carbon methodology
  • Verra VM0033 or VM0047 methodology for macroalgae cultivation
  • Register credit batches annually; sell to carbon offset buyers at USD 20–50/tonne CO₂
  • Year 1 estimated: 5 tonnes CO₂ — modest, but growing with scale

02 Executive Summary
▼

Shoulder Island runs on diesel generators — expensive, polluting, and logistically vulnerable. This venture installs a 30kW solar array with 100kWh battery bank to achieve full energy independence, reducing operating costs by MVR 231–539K/year and qualifying for Gold Standard carbon credits on diesel displacement.

Simultaneously, the island's organic waste streams — coconut husks, crop residues, food scraps, poultry manure — are converted into high-value biochar via pyrolysis kilns (sold at MVR 2,310/tonne to regional farms), premium compost (sold locally), and Black Soldier Fly larvae (BSFL) dried protein meal for poultry and fish feed. Every waste input has an output with a price tag.

03 System Components
▼

Solar + Storage

  • 30kW monocrystalline array — rooftop and ground-mount
  • 100kWh LFP battery bank — 48h+ autonomy without sun
  • Bidirectional inverter for potential grid export
  • Eliminates diesel generator fuel cost entirely

Biochar Kiln

  • Continuous-feed pyrolysis unit processing 200kg/day of crop waste
  • Biochar output: ~50kg/day dry weight
  • Biochar applied to hydroponic grow beds; surplus sold at MVR 2,310/tonne
  • Syngas from pyrolysis used for cooking — displaces LPG

BSF (Black Soldier Fly) Composting

  • BSF larvae consume food scraps and poultry manure in 14-day cycles
  • Larvae harvested, dried → 40% protein meal for poultry + fish feed
  • Residual frass → premium compost sold to regional agriculture
  • System capacity: 50kg waste/day → 10kg protein meal + 15kg compost/day

Carbon Credits

  • Gold Standard methodology for diesel displacement (solar)
  • Verra VCS for biochar carbon sequestration
  • Estimated 150–300 tCO₂e/year across both projects
